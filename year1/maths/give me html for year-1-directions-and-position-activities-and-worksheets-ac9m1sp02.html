About This Year 1 Maths Topic

Direction and position activities help students describe how people and objects move through familiar spaces.

In this unit, students use words such as forwards, backwards, straight ahead, left, right, first, second, beside, before and after to describe movement and location.

Students interpret and give clear directions, create simple algorithms and role-play robots that follow one instruction at a time.

They also use ordinal and positional language to arrange people within a line and describe familiar journeys across Country/Place.

These activities support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9M1SP02 and its associated elaborations.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • understand and use directional language;
  • follow instructions using forwards and backwards;
  • turn left and right correctly;
  • move straight ahead to a stated location;
  • give clear directions to another person;
  • create a simple sequence of movement instructions;
  • follow an algorithm one step at a time;
  • use ordinal and positional language in a line;
  • describe familiar journeys and routes;
  • explain why precise directions are important.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • direction arrow cards;
  • left and right wristbands or stickers;
  • floor grids or masking-tape pathways;
  • cones, hoops and classroom landmarks;
  • toy people, animals or vehicles;
  • robot role-play cards;
  • simple maps of familiar places;
  • ordinal number cards;
  • position word cards;
  • mini whiteboards and markers;
  • journey picture cards;
  • authoritative First Nations Country/Place resources.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Introduce forwards, backwards and straight ahead.
  2. Teach left and right using body-based cues.
  3. Follow one-step movement instructions.
  4. Combine instructions into short routes.
  5. Practise giving directions around familiar spaces.
  6. Introduce algorithms as ordered instruction sets.
  7. Role-play robots that move only when instructed.
  8. Use ordinal and positional words in line-up activities.
  9. Represent and describe familiar journeys.
  10. Encourage students to check, improve and explain directions.

Teacher Questions

  • Which direction should you move first?
  • What does straight ahead mean?
  • How do you know which side is left?
  • What happens after this instruction?
  • Are the directions in the correct order?
  • What would happen if one step were missing?
  • Who is third in the line?
  • Who is standing between these two people?
  • How can you describe the route clearly?
  • Where should the robot stop?
  • Could the directions be shorter or clearer?
  • What landmarks help describe the journey?

Common Misconceptions

Confusing left and right

Use consistent body cues, wristbands or the “L” shape made with the left hand before moving to map-based directions.

Giving vague instructions

Replace words such as “go there” with precise directions that include movement, number of steps and a landmark.

Following instructions out of order

Number each instruction and have students complete one step before reading the next.

Turning instead of moving

Explicitly distinguish between turning to face a direction and moving forwards in that direction.

Confusing ordinal position with quantity

Explain that third tells where someone is in an order, not how many people are present.

Year 1 Directions and Position Activities

Activity 1: Follow the Arrow Path

Arrange arrows on the floor showing forwards, backwards, left and right.

Students follow the route and describe each movement aloud.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E1

Activity 2: Classroom Direction Hunt

Give directions to familiar classroom locations using landmarks and clear movement words.

Students follow the route and identify the destination.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E1

Activity 3: Left or Right?

Call out body movements such as raise your left hand, turn right or step left.

Gradually combine instructions into short sequences.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E1

Activity 4: Robot Algorithm Game

One student acts as a robot and moves only when another student gives a precise instruction.

The class checks whether the instructions reach the target.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E2

Activity 5: Code the Toy

Place a toy on a floor grid and create arrow-card instructions to move it to a destination.

Students test, debug and improve the algorithm.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E2

Activity 6: Missing Instruction

Show a route with one missing step. Students identify and insert the instruction needed to complete the journey.

Discuss how one missing step changes the result.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E2

Activity 7: Line-Up Challenge

Students line up by height and use first, second, third, before, after, beside and between to describe positions.

Change the order and repeat with new instructions.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E3

Activity 8: Position Swap

Give instructions such as “The fourth person moves beside the first person” or “Swap the second and fifth positions.”

Students describe the new line order.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E3

Activity 9: My Familiar Journey

Students draw a simple route between two familiar places and describe the journey using directional language.

Include landmarks that help make the directions clear.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E4

Activity 10: Journey Across Country/Place

Use an appropriate, authoritative resource to explore a familiar journey described through Country/Place.

Discuss landmarks, direction and the importance of local knowledge when describing routes.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP02_E4

Differentiation

Support

  • begin with one-step directions;
  • teach forwards and backwards before left and right;
  • use coloured wristbands for left and right;
  • provide large floor arrows;
  • use familiar classroom landmarks;
  • limit algorithms to two or three steps;
  • provide ordinal number cards;
  • model each direction before independent practice;
  • allow students to point or demonstrate orally.

Extension

  • create longer multi-step algorithms;
  • include obstacles and alternative routes;
  • compare two possible paths to the same destination;
  • debug an incorrect instruction sequence;
  • write directions for another student to test;
  • use simple grid coordinates where appropriate;
  • combine ordinal and directional language;
  • design a classroom treasure hunt;
  • explain why one route is clearer or more efficient.

Assessment Ideas

Observe whether the student can:

  • understand forwards, backwards, left and right;
  • follow one-step and multi-step directions;
  • give clear directional instructions;
  • use landmarks appropriately;
  • create a simple movement algorithm;
  • follow instructions in the correct order;
  • identify and correct an incorrect step;
  • use ordinal and positional language;
  • describe a familiar journey;
  • explain why directions are effective.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Choose a classroom destination.
  2. Write or say three movement instructions.
  3. Ask a partner to follow them.
  4. Explain one change that would make the directions clearer.

Home Learning Ideas

Families can practise directions during walks, household tasks, toy play and simple treasure hunts.

Use familiar landmarks and ask the child to give or follow instructions using forwards, backwards, left, right and straight ahead.

Ask questions such as:

  • Which direction should we move first?
  • Should we turn left or right?
  • What happens next?
  • Can you give me three steps to follow?
  • Where should the toy stop?
  • Who is second in the line?
  • Who is standing beside you?
  • What landmark comes before the turn?
  • Can you describe the journey home?
  • How could you make the directions clearer?
