Year 1 Equal Sharing and Grouping Activities

Build early multiplication and division understanding with engaging Year 1 Equal Sharing and Grouping Activities aligned to Australian Curriculum AC9M1N06. About This Year 1 Maths Topic

Equal sharing and grouping help students build the foundations for multiplication and division. In Year 1, students solve practical problems by making equal groups, sharing collections fairly and checking that every group has the same amount.

Students use physical and virtual materials, diagrams, drawings and spoken explanations to represent repeated equal-group situations. They may investigate questions such as how many wheels are needed for several cars or how many objects belong in each group.

Equal-sharing situations include sharing dominoes, counters, classroom materials or play money between a given number of people. Students count or subitise to confirm that each person receives an equal share.

Money-sharing activities provide an everyday context for sorting coins by denomination, sharing coins fairly and checking that each person receives equal amounts.

These activities support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9M1N06 and its associated elaborations.

Australian Curriculum Alignment

AC9M1N06

Use mathematical modelling to solve practical problems involving equal sharing and grouping. Represent the situations with diagrams, physical and virtual materials, and use calculation strategies to solve the problem.

AC9M1N06_E1

Model repeated equal-group situations, such as determining how many wheels are needed for 3 cars, using materials, drawings and number records.

AC9M1N06_E2

Model practical equal-sharing situations, such as sharing a set of dominoes between 2 players and checking that both players receive the same number of tiles.

AC9M1N06_E3

Model money problems involving equal sharing, including sorting coins by denomination, sharing them equally between people and checking that each person receives equal amounts of each denomination.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • recognise equal groups in practical situations;
  • make repeated groups with the same number of objects;
  • share a collection fairly between people or groups;
  • check that every group has an equal amount;
  • represent grouping problems using materials and drawings;
  • represent equal-sharing problems using diagrams;
  • count or subitise to compare shares;
  • record the result of a grouping or sharing problem;
  • sort and share coins by denomination;
  • explain how their model solves the problem.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • counters, cubes, buttons or bottle caps;
  • toy cars, animals or classroom figures;
  • sorting trays, hoops or small bowls;
  • dominoes and playing cards;
  • equal-sharing mats;
  • equal-group templates;
  • paper plates or cups;
  • play Australian coins;
  • coin-sorting trays;
  • mini whiteboards and markers;
  • picture cards and story problem cards;
  • virtual manipulatives or digital grouping tools.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Begin with practical situations involving equal groups.
  2. Build repeated groups using small collections of objects.
  3. Check that each group contains the same number.
  4. Draw and record the equal groups.
  5. Introduce fair-sharing situations with two recipients.
  6. Share objects one at a time until the collection is used.
  7. Count or subitise each share to check equality.
  8. Extend sharing to three or four recipients.
  9. Introduce money sharing by sorting coins into denominations.
  10. Ask students to explain and justify the solution.

Teacher Questions

  • How many groups do we need?
  • How many objects should be in each group?
  • Are all the groups equal?
  • How can you check?
  • How many objects are there altogether?
  • How could you draw the groups?
  • How many people are sharing?
  • Does everyone have the same amount?
  • Are there any objects left over?
  • How many coins of each type does each person receive?
  • What does your diagram show?
  • How do you know your answer is fair?

Common Misconceptions

Making groups with different numbers of objects

Ask students to count each group and compare them side by side. Use trays or hoops to keep the groups clearly separated.

Sharing several objects with one person before moving on

Model one-at-a-time sharing so each recipient receives one object in turn.

Confusing the number of groups with the number in each group

Use sentence frames such as “There are ___ groups with ___ objects in each group.”

Ignoring leftover objects

Place leftover objects in a separate space and discuss whether the collection can be shared equally.

Sharing coins equally by number but not by denomination

Sort coins by denomination first, then share each type separately and compare the amounts.

Recording only the answer without showing the model

Ask students to connect the materials, diagram and number record to the original situation.

Year 1 Equal Sharing and Grouping Activities

Activity 1: Wheels for Cars

Give students 3 toy cars or car pictures. Ask how many wheels are needed altogether if each car has 4 wheels.

Students build equal groups, draw the cars and record the total number of wheels.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E1

Activity 2: Build Repeated Equal Groups

Give students a number of groups and a group size. Students build the repeated groups using counters or cubes.

They count the total and explain how they know every group is equal.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E1

Activity 3: Draw the Groups

Present a practical grouping problem. Students make the groups with materials, then draw a matching diagram.

They label the number of groups, the number in each group and the total.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E1

Activity 4: Share the Dominoes

Give a set of dominoes to two players. Students share the dominoes one at a time until the set is distributed.

Count or subitise each share to check that both players have the same number.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E2

Activity 5: Teddy Bear Picnic

Share pretend food, plates or cups equally between toy bears.

Students check each bear’s share and record whether any objects are left over.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E2

Activity 6: Fair or Unfair?

Display several sharing arrangements. Some should be equal and others unequal.

Students identify which are fair, correct the unfair shares and explain their reasoning.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E2

Activity 7: Share Between Three or Four

Give students a collection and three or four recipients. Students share the objects one at a time.

They check whether each recipient receives an equal amount.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06

Activity 8: Sort and Share Coins

Give students a mixed collection of play coins. Students sort the coins by denomination before sharing them equally.

They compare the number and value of coins received by each person.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E3

Activity 9: Money Box Challenge

Present a money-box collection to be shared between four people.

Students share each denomination equally and record how many coins of each type each person receives.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06_E3

Activity 10: Create a Sharing Problem

Students create a practical equal-sharing or grouping problem using classroom objects, toys or coins.

A partner models, draws and solves the problem.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1N06

Differentiation

Support

  • begin with two equal groups;
  • use small collections below 12;
  • share objects one at a time;
  • use sorting trays or equal-sharing mats;
  • provide pre-drawn circles for each group;
  • use identical objects before mixed collections;
  • count each group aloud together;
  • provide sentence starters and adult prompting;
  • allow oral explanations before written recording.

Extension

  • use larger collections and more groups;
  • include problems with leftovers;
  • compare two different grouping arrangements;
  • write repeated-addition number sentences;
  • create missing-group or missing-share problems;
  • share mixed coin denominations while preserving equal value;
  • solve two-step grouping and sharing problems;
  • create original problems for classmates;
  • explain why more than one model may be correct.

Assessment Ideas

Observe whether the student can:

  • recognise equal groups;
  • construct repeated groups accurately;
  • share a collection fairly;
  • check that each group has the same number;
  • count or subitise each share;
  • represent grouping with materials and drawings;
  • record the number of groups and group size;
  • identify and discuss leftovers;
  • sort and share coins by denomination;
  • explain how the model solves the problem.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Give the student 12 counters.
  2. Ask the student to share them equally between 3 people.
  3. Draw the groups and record the share.
  4. Explain how they know the sharing is fair.
