About This Year 1 Maths Topic

Shape learning helps students describe, compare and classify the forms they see in everyday objects and the environment.

In this unit, students explore circles, ovals, triangles, quadrilaterals, regular shapes and irregular shapes, and discuss what is the same and different about each group.

They identify shapes by sight and touch, build different objects using the same number of blocks and compare how their constructions differ.

Students also explore string shapes and storytelling contexts, making connections between shapes, objects and Country/Place.

These activities support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9M1SP01 and its associated elaborations.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • recognise familiar shapes in different orientations;
  • name and describe circles, ovals, triangles and quadrilaterals;
  • compare regular and irregular shapes;
  • classify shapes using visible properties;
  • explain similarities and differences between shape groups;
  • identify a shape using touch and descriptive clues;
  • make shapes and objects using blocks or centi-cubes;
  • compare constructions made from the same number of blocks;
  • recognise shapes in the environment;
  • communicate spatial reasoning using appropriate vocabulary.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • 2D shape cards and cut-outs;
  • circles, ovals, triangles and quadrilaterals;
  • regular and irregular shape examples;
  • fabric bags for mystery shape activities;
  • foam, wooden or plastic shapes;
  • blocks and centi-cubes;
  • construction mats;
  • photographs of shapes in the environment;
  • string or yarn for shape making;
  • sorting hoops and labels;
  • mini whiteboards and markers;
  • authoritative First Nations storytelling resources.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Review familiar shape names.
  2. Describe shapes using sides, corners and curved edges.
  3. Sort shapes by one visible property.
  4. Compare regular and irregular shapes.
  5. Discuss similarities within and differences between groups.
  6. Identify hidden shapes through touch.
  7. Build objects using a fixed number of blocks.
  8. Compare constructions made from equal numbers of blocks.
  9. Find and photograph shapes in the environment.
  10. Explore string shapes through respectful storytelling contexts.

Teacher Questions

  • What shape do you see?
  • How many sides does it have?
  • How many corners does it have?
  • Are the sides straight or curved?
  • What is the same about these shapes?
  • What is different between these groups?
  • How can you describe the shape without naming it?
  • What clues helped you identify the hidden shape?
  • How are these block constructions alike?
  • How are they different?
  • Where can you find this shape in the environment?
  • Why did you classify the shape in that group?

Common Misconceptions

Believing a shape changes when it is rotated

Show the same shape in several orientations and discuss which properties remain unchanged.

Classifying shapes only by colour or size

Encourage students to use mathematical properties such as sides, corners, straight edges and curved edges.

Thinking all quadrilaterals look like squares

Provide a wide variety of four-sided shapes, including rectangles, rhombuses, trapeziums and irregular examples.

Confusing objects with shapes

Explain that a shape describes a form, while an object is a real item that may contain one or more familiar shapes.

Believing equal block counts make identical constructions

Build several different arrangements with the same number of blocks and compare their height, width and structure.

Year 1 Shapes and Objects Activities

Activity 1: Shape Sorting Hoops

Give students a mixed collection of circles, ovals, triangles, quadrilaterals and irregular shapes.

Students sort the collection and explain the rule used for each group.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E1

Activity 2: Same and Different

Compare two shape groups and record what is the same within each group and what is different between the groups.

Encourage more than one valid classification rule.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E1

Activity 3: Regular or Irregular?

Present familiar and unfamiliar shapes and discuss whether their sides and angles appear equal or unequal.

Students sort the shapes into regular and irregular groups.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E1

Activity 4: Mystery Shape Bag

Place shapes inside a fabric bag. One student feels a shape without looking and describes its sides, corners and curves.

Classmates name the shape and explain which clues helped.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E2

Activity 5: Shape Clue Challenge

Students write or say clues for a selected shape without using its name.

Partners identify the shape and justify their answer.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E2

Activity 6: Build with Six Blocks

Every student receives the same number of blocks and creates an object or structure.

Compare how the constructions differ even though the number of blocks is the same.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E3

Activity 7: Construction Gallery Walk

Display student constructions and ask classmates to compare height, width, arrangement and shape.

Students describe one similarity and one difference.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E3

Activity 8: Shape Hunt

Search the classroom or school for objects containing circles, ovals, triangles or quadrilaterals.

Record or photograph examples and explain the connection.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01

Activity 9: String Shape Investigation

Use string to make familiar and irregular shapes, then compare, describe and classify each result.

Focus on respectful, age-appropriate connections to storytelling and Country/Place.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E4

Activity 10: Karda and String Story Contexts

Explore authoritative resources relating to Karda from the Yandruwandha Peoples of north-east South Australia.

Discuss shapes represented in the string design and their relationship to objects and Country/Place.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1SP01_E4

Differentiation

Support

  • begin with circles, triangles, squares and rectangles;
  • use large tactile shape pieces;
  • provide picture-word vocabulary cards;
  • sort by one property at a time;
  • use sentence starters for comparisons;
  • provide fewer shapes in the mystery bag;
  • use modelled block constructions;
  • allow oral responses and partner discussion;
  • accept drawings or photographs as evidence.

Extension

  • classify the same shapes in several ways;
  • include unfamiliar quadrilaterals and irregular shapes;
  • create shape riddles for classmates;
  • compare several block constructions in detail;
  • design an object using a fixed number of blocks;
  • find shapes embedded within complex objects;
  • create and explain a new classification rule;
  • record similarities and differences in a table;
  • justify why a shape belongs to more than one possible group.

Assessment Ideas

Observe whether the student can:

  • recognise and name familiar shapes;
  • describe shape properties;
  • classify a mixed shape collection;
  • identify similarities and differences;
  • distinguish regular and irregular shapes;
  • identify a shape using touch;
  • provide useful shape clues;
  • build and compare block constructions;
  • recognise shapes in the environment;
  • explain classification reasoning.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Choose one shape.
  2. Describe its sides, corners and edges.
  3. Place it in a suitable group.
  4. Explain why it belongs there.

Home Learning Ideas

Families can search for familiar shapes in packaging, signs, furniture, toys and household objects.

Use building blocks, shape cut-outs or string to create, compare and classify different shapes and constructions.

Ask questions such as:

  • What shape can you see?
  • How many sides does it have?
  • How many corners does it have?
  • What is the same about these shapes?
  • What is different?
  • Can you describe the shape without naming it?
  • Can you build something using six blocks?
  • How is your construction different from mine?
  • Where can you find this shape at home?
  • How would you classify these objects?
