About This Year 1 Maths Topic

Measurement comparison helps students describe how objects and events are alike and different. In Year 1, students compare and order familiar objects using the attributes of length, mass, capacity and duration.

Students begin with direct comparison, such as placing two objects side by side, before using indirect comparison tools such as string, balance scales, containers and sand timers.

They use comparative language including shortest, longer, longest, lightest, heavier, heaviest, holds the least, holds the most, shorter time and longer time.

Students explain how they made their decisions and communicate the evidence they used to order objects and events.

These activities support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9M1M01 and its associated elaborations.

Australian Curriculum Alignment

AC9M1M01

Compare directly and indirectly and order objects and events using attributes of length, mass, capacity and duration, communicating reasoning.

AC9M1M01_E1

Use a piece of string to indirectly compare the length of several objects, decide which will fit within a space and describe the order using shortest, short, longer and longest.

AC9M1M01_E2

Order the mass of three or more objects using hefting and balance scales, and explain the order using lightest, light, heavier and heaviest.

AC9M1M01_E3

Pour sand, rice or water between containers to compare and order the capacity of three or more containers using holds the most, holds the least and in-between language.

AC9M1M01_E4

Create sand timers from everyday or recycled materials and compare them to order the duration of time required for the sand to run through.

AC9M1M01_E5

Investigate situations where First Nations Australians estimate, compare and communicate measurements, including seasons, animal tracks, water-carrying vessels and rock holes.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • identify length, mass, capacity and duration as measurement attributes;
  • compare two objects directly;
  • compare objects indirectly using a suitable tool;
  • order three or more objects by length;
  • order three or more objects by mass;
  • order three or more containers by capacity;
  • compare and order events by duration;
  • use comparative measurement language correctly;
  • choose an appropriate comparison method;
  • explain and communicate measurement reasoning.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • pieces of string or ribbon;
  • classroom objects of different lengths;
  • balance scales;
  • rocks, blocks, toys and classroom items of different masses;
  • containers of different shapes and sizes;
  • sand, dry rice or water;
  • scoops, funnels and trays;
  • clear bottles and recycled containers;
  • materials for making sand timers;
  • picture cards showing everyday events;
  • measurement word cards;
  • recording tables and mini whiteboards.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Introduce the four measurement attributes.
  2. Compare two objects directly by placing them together.
  3. Use string to compare lengths indirectly.
  4. Order three or more objects from shortest to longest.
  5. Compare mass by hefting objects in each hand.
  6. Confirm mass comparisons using balance scales.
  7. Compare capacity by transferring the same material.
  8. Order containers from least to greatest capacity.
  9. Compare durations using actions and sand timers.
  10. Encourage students to explain their evidence and reasoning.

Teacher Questions

  • Which object is longer?
  • How can we compare these without moving them?
  • Which object is the shortest?
  • How do you know this object is heavier?
  • What happened to the balance scale?
  • Which container holds the most?
  • Did the tallest container hold the most?
  • Which event took the longest?
  • What tool helped you compare?
  • Can you place these objects in order?
  • What measurement words describe your result?
  • What evidence supports your answer?

Common Misconceptions

Assuming taller always means greater capacity

Compare tall narrow containers with short wide containers by transferring the same material between them.

Comparing objects from different starting points

Align one end of each object before making a direct length comparison.

Using size to judge mass

Include large light objects and small heavy objects so students learn that size and mass are different attributes.

Believing an object higher on the balance is heavier

Demonstrate that the heavier side moves down and the lighter side rises.

Changing the amount poured during capacity comparisons

Use the same material and transfer the full amount carefully so the comparison remains fair.

Confusing clock time with duration

Explain that duration means how long an event takes, not the time shown on a clock.

Year 1 Measurement Comparison Activities

Activity 1: Direct Length Comparison

Place two classroom objects side by side with one end aligned. Students decide which is shorter and which is longer.

Repeat with several object pairs and record the comparison.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01

Activity 2: String Length Detective

Use one piece of string to compare the length of objects that cannot easily be moved or placed together.

Students order the objects from shortest to longest.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E1

Activity 3: Will It Fit?

Students use string to test whether books, boxes or classroom objects will fit inside a chosen space.

They explain how the string helped them decide.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E1

Activity 4: Heft and Predict

Students hold one object in each hand and predict which is heavier before checking with a balance scale.

Compare the prediction with the scale result.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E2

Activity 5: Balance Scale Order

Compare three or more objects with a balance scale and place them in order from lightest to heaviest.

Students explain what happened on the scale.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E2

Activity 6: Which Container Holds More?

Fill one container with rice, sand or water and pour it into another container to compare capacity.

Students describe which holds more and which holds less.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E3

Activity 7: Capacity Ordering Challenge

Compare three or more containers and order them from the least capacity to the greatest capacity.

Discuss why appearance alone may be misleading.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E3

Activity 8: Make a Sand Timer

Create simple sand timers using clear recycled containers and compare how long the sand takes to pass through.

Order the timers from shortest to longest duration.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E4

Activity 9: Event Duration Sort

Sort familiar activities such as blinking, washing hands, eating lunch and sleeping by how long they take.

Students justify the order using duration language.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01

Activity 10: First Nations Measurement Investigation

Explore authoritative resources about seasonal knowledge, animal tracks, traditional water-carrying vessels and rock holes.

Use respectful, community-approved sources and focus on how measurement knowledge supports decision-making.

Curriculum connection: AC9M1M01_E5

Differentiation

Support

  • compare only two objects at first;
  • use highly contrasting objects;
  • provide visual vocabulary cards;
  • model how to align objects before comparing length;
  • use clear containers for capacity tasks;
  • provide labelled light and heavy baskets;
  • use familiar short and long events;
  • provide partially completed ordering strips;
  • allow oral explanations or adult scribing.

Extension

  • compare more than four objects or events;
  • include objects with very similar measurements;
  • ask students to choose their own comparison tool;
  • find objects that challenge visual assumptions;
  • design a fair capacity investigation;
  • create and test a new sand timer;
  • compare two attributes of the same objects;
  • record results in a table;
  • justify why one method is more reliable than another.

Assessment Ideas

Observe whether the student can:

  • identify the attribute being compared;
  • compare two objects directly;
  • use string for indirect length comparison;
  • order objects from shortest to longest;
  • use hefting and balance scales appropriately;
  • order objects from lightest to heaviest;
  • compare and order container capacities;
  • compare and order durations;
  • use comparative measurement language;
  • explain the evidence used to make a decision.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Choose three classroom objects.
  2. Order them by one measurement attribute.
  3. State the comparison words you used.
  4. Explain how you checked your order.

Home Learning Ideas

Families can compare household objects such as spoons, books, shoes, containers, toys and food packages using length, mass, capacity and duration.

Use a piece of string, two hands for hefting, safe containers and familiar household events to make measurement practical.

Ask questions such as:

  • Which object is longer?
  • Which object is the shortest?
  • How could we compare these indirectly?
  • Which object feels heavier?
  • How could we check?
  • Which container holds more?
  • Does the tallest container hold the most?
  • Which activity takes longer?
  • Can you place these in order?
  • How do you know your order is correct?
