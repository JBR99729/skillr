About This Foundation Maths Topic

Understanding time in Foundation begins with familiar routines rather than clocks and formal units. Children learn to describe when events happen during the day and how days follow one another in a repeating weekly sequence.

Students connect everyday activities with morning, lunchtime, afternoon and night. They order pictures, discuss daily routines and explain why an event belongs in a particular part of the day.

Students also distinguish between school days and weekends, sequence events from stories and interpret classroom rosters using words such as yesterday, today and tomorrow.

These teaching ideas and printable resources support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9MFM02 and its associated elaborations.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • name the days of the week in order;
  • recognise that the weekly sequence repeats;
  • identify school days and weekend days;
  • connect familiar events with different times of the day;
  • sequence daily routines in a sensible order;
  • sequence events from a familiar story;
  • use the words first, next, then and last;
  • use yesterday, today and tomorrow correctly;
  • read and discuss a simple classroom roster;
  • create a pictorial diary of weekly events;
  • explain why an event belongs in a particular sequence.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • days-of-the-week cards;
  • daily routine picture cards;
  • story sequencing cards;
  • string, pegs or magnetic display strips;
  • classroom calendar;
  • weekly timetable or roster;
  • morning, lunchtime, afternoon and night labels;
  • picture books with clear event sequences;
  • drawing paper and pencils;
  • pictorial diary templates;
  • classroom job cards;
  • photographs of familiar school activities.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Begin with familiar events from a typical school day.
  2. Introduce the terms morning, lunchtime, afternoon and night using pictures and classroom routines.
  3. Ask students to sort familiar events into the correct time of day.
  4. Introduce the days of the week using songs, cards and a classroom calendar.
  5. Highlight the difference between school days and weekend days.
  6. Demonstrate that the weekly sequence repeats after Sunday.
  7. Sequence events from a familiar story using picture cards.
  8. Model language such as first, next, then and last.
  9. Introduce yesterday, today and tomorrow through daily calendar discussions.
  10. Use a classroom roster to discuss turns, responsibilities and repeating weekly routines.
  11. Support students to create a pictorial diary showing important weekly events.

Teacher Questions

  • What happens first in your day?
  • What usually happens in the morning?
  • What happens around lunchtime?
  • What do you usually do in the afternoon?
  • What happens at night?
  • Which event should come next?
  • How do you know this event belongs here?
  • What day is it today?
  • What day was yesterday?
  • What day will it be tomorrow?
  • Which days are school days?
  • Which days make up the weekend?
  • What day comes after Sunday?
  • What happened first in the story?
  • What happened next?
  • Whose turn is it today?
  • Who completed the job yesterday?
  • Who will complete the job tomorrow?

Common Misconceptions

Believing the week ends permanently on Sunday

Arrange the days in a circle and show that Monday follows Sunday as the weekly sequence repeats.

Confusing afternoon and night

Use familiar examples such as going home from school in the afternoon and going to bed at night.

Using yesterday and tomorrow interchangeably

Refer to a visible classroom calendar each day and move a marker to show yesterday, today and tomorrow.

Sequencing pictures by preference instead of time order

Ask students to explain what happened before and after each event rather than choosing the picture they like most.

Thinking all children have identical daily routines

Explain that routines can differ between families while events can still be discussed using times of the day.

Believing all weekdays are school days

Discuss the local school week and clearly distinguish it from the broader group of weekdays.

Foundation Days and Daily Routines Activities

Activity 1: Daily Event String Line

Stretch a string line across the room and label sections morning, lunchtime, afternoon and night.

Students peg pictures of familiar events onto the line and explain why each event belongs in that position.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E1

Activity 2: Sort the Times of the Day

Provide picture cards showing events such as waking up, eating lunch, playing after school and going to bed.

Students sort the cards under morning, lunchtime, afternoon or night.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E1

Activity 3: My School Day Sequence

Students arrange photographs or drawings of school events, such as arriving, morning learning, lunch, afternoon learning and going home.

Students describe the order using first, next, then and last.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E1

Activity 4: Build the Week

Give students cards showing the seven days of the week. Students place the cards in the correct sequence.

Join the first and last cards to demonstrate that the weekly sequence repeats.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E2

Activity 5: School Day or Weekend?

Students sort the days of the week into school days and weekend days according to the school timetable.

Discuss activities that commonly happen during each part of the week.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E2

Activity 6: What Day Comes Next?

Display one day card and ask students to identify the day that comes before and the day that comes after it.

Include questions such as “What comes after Sunday?” to reinforce the repeating sequence.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E2

Activity 7: Story Sequencing Cards

Read a familiar story and provide pictures showing its major events.

Students place the pictures in order and retell the story using first, next, then and last.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E3

Activity 8: Mix and Fix the Story

Display three or four story events in the wrong order.

Students identify the problem, rearrange the events and explain how they know the correct sequence.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E3

Activity 9: Classroom Job Roster

Create a simple weekly roster for classroom jobs such as watering plants, tidying books or feeding a class pet.

Ask students who completed the job yesterday, whose turn it is today and who will complete it tomorrow.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E4

Activity 10: Today, Yesterday and Tomorrow

Use the classroom calendar to identify today's day and date.

Students move labels to show yesterday, today and tomorrow and describe one event connected with each day.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E4

Activity 11: My Weekly Picture Diary

Students create a seven-day pictorial diary showing an important event for each day.

They may draw school activities, family routines, sport, appointments or weekend events.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E5

Activity 12: Class Weekly Diary

Work together to create a large class diary showing regular events such as library visits, sport, assemblies and classroom jobs.

Refer to the diary throughout the week to discuss what happened yesterday and what will happen next.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFM02_E5

Differentiation

Support

  • use clear photographs of familiar events;
  • begin with two or three events rather than a full sequence;
  • use visual labels for each time of the day;
  • sing days-of-the-week songs regularly;
  • display a permanent weekly calendar;
  • use colour-coded school-day and weekend cards;
  • provide sentence starters for oral explanations;
  • allow students to sequence events through role-play.

Extension

  • sequence longer stories with more events;
  • ask students to create their own sequencing cards;
  • compare two different daily routines;
  • identify repeated events across several days;
  • create a personal or classroom weekly timetable;
  • explain what happens before and after a nominated event;
  • use calendar dates alongside days of the week;
  • write short captions for pictorial diary entries.

  • identify morning, lunchtime, afternoon and night;
  • match familiar events with an appropriate time of day;
  • sequence daily events correctly;
  • sequence events from a familiar story;
  • use first, next, then and last correctly;
  • use yesterday, today and tomorrow correctly;
  • interpret a simple classroom roster;
  • create a pictorial weekly diary;
  • justify the placement of an event in a sequence.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Name today's day.
  2. Name the day that comes after today.
  3. Place three daily event pictures in order.
  4. Explain which event happens first and which happens last.

Home Learning Ideas

Families can practise sequencing by discussing familiar daily routines, weekly activities and events from stories.

Children can draw pictures of waking up, eating meals, playing, going to school and getting ready for bed, then place them in order.

Ask questions such as:

  • What happens first in your day?
  • What happens after breakfast?
  • What do you usually do in the afternoon?
  • What happens at night?
  • What day is it today?
  • What day was yesterday?
  • What day will it be tomorrow?
  • Which days are part of the weekend?
  • What regular event happens on this day?
  • Can you retell this story in the correct order?
