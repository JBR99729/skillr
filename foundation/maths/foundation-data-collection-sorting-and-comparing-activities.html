Foundation Data Collection, Sorting and Comparing Activities

Help Foundation students collect, sort, organise, compare and interpret simple data using familiar objects, pictures, classroom charts and investigative questions. Children explore early statistics through practical sorting games, yes-or-no questions, role-play activities and printable worksheets.

View Teacher Guide Explore Activities View Resources

About This Foundation Maths Topic

Statistics in Foundation Year begins with familiar questions, everyday objects and simple visual representations. Students learn that data is information collected to help answer a question.

Children collect information by observing, asking questions, counting and sorting objects or pictures. They organise the information into groups so that it can be compared and discussed.

Students explore questions such as, “Which toy is most common?”, “Do more children have shoes with laces or without laces?” and “Were there more tigers or kangaroos in the story?”

These learning experiences develop early data literacy, classification, comparison, reasoning, communication and problem-solving skills.

The teaching ideas and printable resources on this page support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9MFST01 and its associated elaborations.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • understand that data is information;
  • collect information to answer a familiar question;
  • sort objects into meaningful groups;
  • explain how objects have been sorted;
  • identify similarities and differences between objects;
  • organise data into categories;
  • answer simple yes-or-no investigative questions;
  • use one-to-one matching to compare two groups;
  • represent information using objects, pictures or stickers;
  • create simple classroom charts and rosters;
  • identify which group has more;
  • identify which group has fewer;
  • identify when two groups have the same number;
  • describe what a simple data display shows;
  • follow instructions for sorting objects;
  • ask useful yes-or-no questions about a mystery object;
  • collect and compare information from a story;
  • recognise that environmental information can help people predict weather events;
  • communicate findings using simple mathematical language.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • classroom toys and familiar objects;
  • blocks, counters, buttons and bottle tops;
  • toy animals and vehicles;
  • shape and colour cards;
  • sorting hoops, baskets or containers;
  • picture cards;
  • sticky notes and stickers;
  • large chart paper;
  • classroom photographs;
  • yes-and-no response cards;
  • storybooks containing animals, people or repeated objects;
  • weather photographs and symbols;
  • simple recording sheets;
  • digital camera or tablet where available;
  • robot costume pieces or simple robot signs.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Begin with familiar objects that students can touch, move and examine.
  2. Ask students to describe similarities and differences between the objects.
  3. Model sorting objects according to one clear feature, such as colour, shape, size or whether the object moves.
  4. Ask students to explain the rule used to create each group.
  5. Demonstrate that the same collection can be sorted in more than one way.
  6. Introduce a simple investigative question about the class or familiar objects.
  7. Collect answers using physical objects, picture cards, names, stickers or hands raised.
  8. Organise the collected information into clearly labelled groups.
  9. Compare groups using one-to-one matching before relying only on counting.
  10. Create a simple classroom chart or roster using one sticker or picture for each response.
  11. Ask students to describe what the chart shows using words such as more, fewer, most, least and equal.
  12. Use robot role-play to reinforce precise sorting instructions.
  13. Introduce mystery-object activities that require students to ask useful yes-or-no questions.
  14. Collect and compare data from a familiar story.
  15. Explore how observations from the environment can provide useful information about weather.
  16. Assess students through practical sorting, oral explanations and simple chart interpretation.

Common Misconceptions

Sorting without a clear rule

Students may place objects into groups without being able to explain why. Ask them to finish the sentence, “These belong together because...”

Changing the sorting rule halfway through

Encourage students to select one feature at a time. For example, sort all objects by colour before trying a new sort based on shape.

Thinking there is only one correct way to sort

Demonstrate that one collection can be sorted in several valid ways, provided the sorting rule is clear and consistent.

Judging more by the physical length of a row

A widely spaced row may look longer even when it contains fewer objects. Use one-to-one matching or align objects carefully before comparing.

Counting pictures without understanding what they represent

Explain that each picture, sticker or object represents one response or one item in the collected data.

Confusing fewer with more

Use paired comparisons and complete sentences such as, “There are more red blocks than blue blocks.”

Asking questions that cannot be answered with yes or no

Model the difference between “What colour is it?” and “Is it red?” before beginning mystery-object activities.

Treating artificial intelligence as human thinking

Explain simply that an artificial intelligence system uses information and examples to recognise patterns and make a guess.

Foundation Data and Statistics Activities

Activity 1: Sort the Toy Collection

Provide a mixed collection of toys, such as cars, animals, balls, blocks and dolls.

Ask students to sort the toys into groups and explain the sorting rule they used.

Possible categories include toys that move and toys that do not move, soft and hard toys, or toys with wheels and toys without wheels.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E1

Activity 2: Sort It Another Way

After students complete one sort, ask them to mix the objects and sort the same collection using a different rule.

Discuss how one object may belong to different groups depending on the question being investigated.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E1

Activity 3: Classroom Object Hunt

Students search the classroom for objects that match a particular category, such as objects made from paper, objects that are round or objects used for writing.

Collect or record the items, then sort and compare the results.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E1

Activity 4: Do Our Shoes Have Laces?

Ask the investigative question, “Do more people in our class have shoes with laces than shoes without laces?”

Students organise themselves or picture cards into two groups and match the groups one-to-one.

Discuss which group has more, fewer or whether the groups are equal.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E2

Activity 5: Yes-or-No Class Survey

Select a familiar yes-or-no question, such as, “Did you eat breakfast today?” or “Do you have a pet?”

Students place their name, picture or counter into the yes or no group.

Compare the two groups and describe the result.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E2

Activity 6: One-to-One Data Matching

Create two rows of objects representing two responses or categories.

Ask students to match one object from the first row with one object from the second row.

Any objects left unmatched show which group has more.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E2

Activity 7: Favourite Fruit Sticker Chart

Display pictures of familiar fruits and give each student one sticker.

Students place their sticker beside their favourite fruit.

Compare the columns and discuss which fruit received the most, least or equal numbers of votes.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E3

Activity 8: Classroom Attendance Roster

Create a simple daily chart where students add a picture, name card or sticker to show that they are present.

Compare attendance across different days and discuss what the chart shows.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E3

Activity 9: Weather Picture Chart

Record the daily weather using picture symbols for sunny, cloudy, rainy, windy or stormy conditions.

At the end of the week, compare the symbols and identify which type of weather occurred most or least often.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E3

Activity 10: Robot Sorting Factory

One student role-plays a robot working in a factory or classroom packing station.

Give the robot a precise instruction such as, “Place all red objects in this box,” or “Sort the products by shape.”

The class checks whether the robot followed the sorting rule correctly.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E4

Activity 11: Debug the Sorting Robot

Deliberately place one or more objects into the wrong group.

Students identify the mistake, explain why the object does not belong and correct the robot's sorting.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E4

Activity 12: Sort by Colour, Shape or Size

Give students a mixed collection of classroom items or picture cards.

Call out one sorting instruction at a time, such as colour, shape, size or function.

Compare how the groups change when the sorting instruction changes.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E4

Activity 13: Train the Mystery Object AI

Hide or select a familiar object without showing it to the class.

Students ask yes-or-no questions such as, “Is it round?”, “Is it made of plastic?” or “Can it move?”

Record the answers and use the collected information to make the best guess.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E5

Activity 14: Useful and Unhelpful Questions

Present several possible questions for identifying a mystery object.

Students decide which questions provide useful information and which questions are too broad, repeated or unrelated.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E5

Activity 15: Object Recognition Sort

Show students several examples of objects from one category, such as cups, shoes, balls or books.

Discuss common features and use those features to decide whether new objects belong to the same category.

Explain that artificial intelligence systems also use examples and features to recognise patterns.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E5

Activity 16: Story Animal Investigation

Read a story containing several types of animals, such as The Waterhole by Graeme Base or another appropriate classroom text.

Ask students to identify the different animals and record each type using pictures, counters or tally objects.

Compare which animals appeared more, fewer or the same number of times.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E6

Activity 17: Story Character Chart

After reading a familiar story, create categories for the characters, objects or settings.

Students place picture cards into the correct categories and answer questions about the completed chart.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E6

Activity 18: Which Appeared More?

Select two characters, animals or objects from a story and ask students to compare how many times each appeared.

Students use picture cards or counters to represent the information before making a comparison.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E6

Activity 19: Environmental Weather Clues

Explore age-appropriate examples of environmental signs that may provide information about changing weather, such as clouds, wind, animal behaviour, plant changes or seasonal patterns.

Discuss how people observe, collect and remember this information over time.

Use culturally appropriate resources and acknowledge the relevant First Nations Peoples and knowledge holders.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E7

Activity 20: Weather Observation Chart

Observe the environment at the same time each day and record information about clouds, wind, temperature sensations and animal activity.

Compare observations across several days and discuss whether any patterns can be identified.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFST01_E7

Differentiation

Support

  • begin with only two clearly different categories;
  • use real objects before introducing picture representations;
  • model the sorting rule aloud;
  • provide category labels with pictures;
  • use small collections of four to six objects;
  • allow students to point or move objects before explaining;
  • use one-to-one matching to compare groups;
  • provide sentence starters;
  • ask one simple comparison question at a time;
  • use familiar classroom contexts;
  • pair students with a supportive partner;
  • repeat the meaning of each picture or sticker in a chart.

Extension

  • sort the same collection in three or more ways;
  • create a new investigative question;
  • design a class survey;
  • predict which category will have the most responses;
  • compare the prediction with the collected data;
  • explain why two students may use different valid sorting rules;
  • create category labels independently;
  • represent the same data using objects and pictures;
  • write or dictate two statements about a completed chart;
  • identify questions that cannot be answered by the data;
  • design instructions for a sorting robot;
  • create a mystery-object questioning game;
  • compare data from two different days or stories.

Assessment Ideas

Observe whether the student can:

  • collect information in response to a question;
  • sort familiar objects into groups;
  • state the sorting rule;
  • sort the same collection in another valid way;
  • recognise when an object does not belong;
  • organise yes-or-no responses;
  • compare two groups using one-to-one matching;
  • identify more and fewer correctly;
  • recognise equal groups;
  • represent one response with one object, picture or sticker;
  • read a simple classroom chart;
  • identify the most and least common category;
  • make a statement supported by the data;
  • follow a robot sorting instruction;
  • ask a useful yes-or-no question;
  • collect information from a familiar story;
  • compare two groups represented by images;
  • describe how environmental observations provide information.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Sort six objects into two groups.
  2. Explain the sorting rule.
  3. Identify which group has more or whether the groups are equal.
  4. State one thing the data shows.

Home Learning Ideas

Families can practise early statistics by sorting familiar household objects, asking simple questions and comparing groups.

Children could sort socks by colour, toys by type, cutlery by shape, groceries by category or books by size. Ask the child to explain the rule used for each group.

Families can also create a simple chart showing daily weather, favourite foods or the number of different objects found during a walk.

Useful questions include:

  • How did you sort these objects?
  • What is the same about the objects in this group?
  • Could you sort them another way?
  • Which group has more?
  • Which group has fewer?
  • Are any groups equal?
  • How could we check?
  • What does our chart show?
  • Which category has the most?
  • Which category has the least?
  • What yes-or-no question could we ask?
  • What information would help us identify a mystery object?
  • What weather signs can you observe outside?
