Foundation Shapes and Shape Sorting Activities

Help Foundation students sort, name, create and describe familiar shapes. Children explore circles, squares, triangles and rectangles, identify shapes in everyday objects and explain how shapes can be grouped according to their features.

View Teacher Guide Explore Activities View Resources

About This Foundation Maths Topic

Shape learning in Foundation begins with handling, sorting, creating and discussing familiar shapes. Students learn that shapes can be recognised by their features rather than only by their colour, size or position.

Children explore familiar two-dimensional shapes such as circles, squares, triangles and rectangles. They sort collections, create shape pictures, form shapes using their bodies and identify shapes within familiar objects.

Students also observe shapes in natural and built environments, including shapes found on Country and Place. They explain their sorting choices and give reasons for identifying a particular shape.

These teaching ideas and printable resources support the Australian Curriculum Version 9.0 content descriptor AC9MFSP01 and its associated elaborations.

Learning Intentions

Students will learn to:

  • recognise and name familiar two-dimensional shapes;
  • identify circles, squares, triangles and rectangles;
  • sort shapes according to observable features;
  • sort shapes by number of sides, colour or size;
  • explain how a collection of shapes has been sorted;
  • create familiar shapes using different materials;
  • create pictures using combinations of shapes;
  • form shapes using their bodies or groups of people;
  • identify shapes within everyday objects;
  • recognise shapes when their size or orientation changes;
  • observe and describe shapes in natural environments;
  • give reasons for identifying or grouping a shape.

Teacher Guide

Recommended Materials

  • wooden, plastic or cardboard shape sets;
  • circle, square, triangle and rectangle cards;
  • sorting hoops or trays;
  • everyday classroom objects;
  • toy vehicles and building blocks;
  • paper shapes for collage activities;
  • drawing and tracing materials;
  • craft sticks, string and modelling clay;
  • photographs of familiar objects and places;
  • natural objects such as leaves, stones and seed pods;
  • large ropes or chalk for body-shape activities;
  • magnifying glasses or digital cameras.

Suggested Teaching Sequence

  1. Begin with familiar objects and invite students to describe what they notice about their shapes.
  2. Introduce circles, squares, triangles and rectangles using large examples that students can handle.
  3. Discuss visible features such as straight sides, curved edges and corners.
  4. Sort shapes by one feature, such as colour, and then sort the same shapes using a different feature.
  5. Ask students to explain the rule used for each sorting group.
  6. Rotate and resize familiar shapes to show that their names do not change.
  7. Search for familiar shapes within classroom objects and photographs.
  8. Create pictures using cut-out or traced shapes.
  9. Form shapes using craft materials, bodies or groups of people.
  10. Explore shapes found outdoors, in natural environments and on Country and Place.
  11. Encourage students to justify their shape identifications using observable features.

Teacher Questions

  • What shape is this?
  • How do you know?
  • How many sides can you see?
  • Does the shape have straight or curved edges?
  • How many corners does it have?
  • What is the same about these shapes?
  • What is different about them?
  • How could we sort these shapes?
  • Can you sort them in another way?
  • Why did you place this shape in this group?
  • Is it still the same shape when it is turned?
  • Can you find this shape in the classroom?
  • Which shapes can you see in this object?
  • Which shapes did you use in your picture?
  • Why did you choose that shape?
  • Can your group create a circle?
  • What shapes can you see outside?
  • What shapes can you observe on Country or Place?

Common Misconceptions

Believing a shape changes when it is rotated

Turn a square into a diamond-like position and discuss how it remains a square because its defining features have not changed.

Naming shapes according to colour or size

Use several examples of the same shape in different colours and sizes to show that these features do not determine the shape name.

Believing every four-sided shape is a square

Compare squares and rectangles and focus on their observable similarities and differences using age-appropriate language.

Recognising only regular or perfectly drawn shapes

Include shapes drawn in different sizes, orientations and slightly varied forms while keeping the defining features clear.

Confusing a three-dimensional object with a flat shape

Explain that a flat circle may be seen on the face of a cylindrical container, but the entire object is not a circle.

Sorting without being able to explain the rule

Ask students to complete the sentence, “I put these shapes together because they all have...”

Foundation Shapes and Shape Sorting Activities

Activity 1: Shape Sorting Hoops

Place a collection of shapes in the centre of the room and provide sorting hoops labelled with different rules.

Students sort the shapes by colour, size, number of sides or shape name and explain the rule they followed.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E1

Activity 2: Sort It Another Way

After students sort a collection using one feature, ask them to reorganise the same collection using a different rule.

Discuss how the same shape can belong to different groups depending on the sorting feature.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E1

Activity 3: Mystery Sorting Rule

The teacher secretly chooses a sorting rule and places several shapes into two groups.

Students observe the groups, predict the rule and give reasons for their answers.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E1

Activity 4: Shape Picture Collage

Provide cut-out circles, squares, triangles and rectangles. Students combine the shapes to create a picture.

Students name the shapes they used and explain why each shape suited part of the picture.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E2

Activity 5: Trace Around Objects

Students trace around classroom objects such as lids, blocks, boxes and tiles.

They compare the outlines and identify the familiar shapes they have created.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E2

Activity 6: Build a Shape

Students use craft sticks, string, pipe cleaners or modelling clay to create familiar shapes.

Ask them to describe the sides, corners or curved edges of each shape.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E2

Activity 7: Human Shape Formation

Small groups use their bodies to form circles, triangles, squares or rectangles.

Students discuss how many people were needed and how they arranged themselves to show each shape.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E3

Activity 8: Rope Shapes

Place a long rope on the floor and invite groups to reshape it into familiar two-dimensional shapes.

Students check whether the rope shows straight sides, curved edges or corners.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E3

Activity 9: Classroom Shape Hunt

Students search the classroom for circles, squares, triangles and rectangles within everyday objects.

They draw or photograph each example and explain which part of the object shows the shape.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E4

Activity 10: Shapes in Toy Vehicles

Examine toy cars, bicycles, trucks or construction vehicles.

Students identify shapes in wheels, windows, signs, frames and other component parts.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E4

Activity 11: Shape Detective

Display a photograph of a kitchen, playground, street or classroom scene.

Students locate familiar shapes and justify each answer by referring to sides, corners or curved edges.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E4

Activity 12: Nature Shape Walk

Walk around the school grounds or a familiar outdoor area and observe shapes in leaves, rocks, flowers, paths and human-made structures.

Students sketch or photograph examples and discuss their shape features.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E5

Activity 13: Shapes on Country and Place

Observe appropriate photographs, natural materials or local surroundings and identify familiar shapes.

Students recreate selected shapes through drawing, tracing, modelling or arranging natural materials.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E5

Activity 14: Natural Object Sorting

Collect suitable natural objects such as leaves, stones, seed pods or bark pieces.

Students sort the objects according to their approximate shapes and explain their grouping choices.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01_E5

Activity 15: Shape Museum

Create a classroom display containing drawings, photographs, models and objects representing familiar shapes.

Students label the shapes and give short oral explanations of why each example belongs in its group.

Curriculum connection: AC9MFSP01

Differentiation

Support

  • begin with circles, squares and triangles;
  • use large shapes that students can handle and trace;
  • provide shape-name cards with clear visual examples;
  • sort according to one obvious feature at a time;
  • use real objects before moving to pictures;
  • provide verbal sentence starters;
  • allow students to respond through pointing or matching;
  • repeat shape hunts in familiar environments;
  • outline sides and corners while naming them.

Extension

  • sort shapes using two features simultaneously;
  • create a sorting rule for classmates to identify;
  • compare squares and rectangles;
  • identify shapes shown in unusual orientations;
  • create complex pictures using several shapes;
  • record the number of each shape found during a hunt;
  • design an object using only nominated shapes;
  • describe shapes without saying their names;
  • identify shapes within more complex environmental images.

Assessment Ideas

Observe whether the student can:

  • recognise and name familiar shapes;
  • identify circles, squares, triangles and rectangles;
  • recognise shapes shown in different sizes and orientations;
  • sort shapes according to a nominated feature;
  • create their own sorting rule;
  • describe how a collection has been sorted;
  • create a familiar shape using materials;
  • use shapes to create a picture;
  • identify shapes within everyday objects;
  • identify shapes within natural environments;
  • describe observable shape features;
  • give a reason for identifying or grouping a shape.

Quick Exit Ticket

  1. Name a circle, square, triangle and rectangle.
  2. Sort four shapes into two groups.
  3. Explain the sorting rule.
  4. Identify one shape within a classroom object.

Home Learning Ideas

Families can explore familiar shapes by looking at household objects, packaging, toys, furniture, signs and outdoor features.

Children can draw or photograph objects containing circles, squares, triangles and rectangles. They can then sort their examples according to shape.

Ask questions such as:

  • What shape can you see?
  • How do you know which shape it is?
  • How many sides does it have?
  • How many corners can you find?
  • Does it have straight or curved edges?
  • Can you find another object with the same shape?
  • Can you sort these objects into shape groups?
  • Can you create a picture using only shapes?
  • Can you find shapes outside or in nature?
  • Does the shape stay the same when you turn it?
